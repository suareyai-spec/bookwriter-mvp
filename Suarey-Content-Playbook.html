Suarey — Content Playbook (Anti-Cringe Edition)

The Aesthetic

Virgil's superpower wasn't being flashy. It was being intentional about everything while making it look effortless. He didn't explain himself. He let the work speak. He put quotes around things. He made you feel like you walked into something already in progress.

That's your energy. You're not "building a brand." You're already living. The camera just happens to be there.

Visual rules:

  • Natural light. Always. No ring lights, no studio setups for social content. Miami golden hour is your best friend.

  • Slightly desaturated warm tones — not the orange-and-teal Instagram filter look. Think film photography. A little grain is good.

  • Negative space. Don't fill the frame. A piece of jewelry on a marble counter with 60% of the frame empty is more luxurious than a close-up.

  • Hands in frame. Virgil was always photographed making things, touching things, drawing things. Your hands cutting an emerald, spinning a record, breaking bread — that's the recurring visual motif.

  • No selfies as the main content. You should be caught in the frame, not posing for it. Someone else filming you, or the camera set up like you forgot it was there.


Tone rules:
  • Never explain why something is cool. If you have to explain it, it's not cool.

  • Captions should be short. 1-2 sentences max, or just a location pin. Let the visual do the work.

  • No "excited to announce" or "so grateful for this opportunity." That's LinkedIn energy. You just post the thing.

  • Quotes around words. Virgil made this iconic. Putting "emerald" or "home" or "research" in quotes makes mundane things feel conceptual.

  • Lowercase everything in captions. No exclamation marks.


Music Content (Not Cringe)

What works:


The silent studio clip. Camera on a tripod behind you. You're at the laptop/controller. No talking. The track plays. Maybe you nod. Cut to black. 15 seconds. Caption: the track name and nothing else.

The one-take set moment. Not a full DJ set. Not a "check out my mix" promo. One moment — the 30 seconds where the room shifts. The drop, the crowd reacting, you not even looking up. Someone else films this on their phone. Raw. A little shaky. That's the point.

The record dig. Filming yourself flipping through vinyl at a shop. No talking. Just the sound of records sliding. You pull one out, put it on, nod. That's the post.

The track comparison. Side-by-side audio of an early version vs. the final. No narration. Just "before" and "after" text. Let people hear the evolution.

The venue before anyone arrives. Empty room. Your gear set up. One light on. Caption: just the venue name and time. Builds anticipation without begging for attendance.

What to avoid:


  • "NEW TRACK OUT NOW link in bio!!!!" — death

  • Filming yourself dancing to your own music

  • Explaining your creative process in a talking-head video

  • "What genre would you call this?" engagement bait

  • Countdown posts to a release

  • Reposting Spotify stats ("thanks for 50 streams!")


Casa de Celadon Content (Not Cringe)

What works:


The process shot. An emerald being set into gold. Close-up. No music, just the ambient sound of the workshop. You don't need to be in frame. The craftsmanship speaks.

The flat lay on unexpected surfaces. A ring on a concrete ledge. A necklace on a DJ controller. Earrings on a restaurant menu. The juxtaposition of luxury stone + everyday surface is the whole Virgil move — elevating the ordinary.

"Research." A photo of rough emerald stones on a table with a loupe and a notebook. Caption: "research." That's it. You're studying gemology. You don't need to make a carousel about it.

The heritage post. A photo from Colombia — the mine, the market, the mountains. No explanation. Maybe a GPS pin. People will ask in the comments. Answer them there. Let curiosity do the marketing.

Wearing it without mentioning it. Post a food Reel wearing a Celadon piece. Don't tag it. Don't mention it. Just wear it. When people comment "what's that ring" — now you have a conversation. This is how Virgil sold Off-White. He wore it. People noticed. He never had to pitch.

The detail shot. One extreme close-up of the emerald's inclusions (the internal "garden" of the stone). These are legitimately stunning under magnification. Caption: "jardin." Gemology term for the natural inclusions in an emerald. Educates without being educational.

What to avoid:


  • "Introducing our new collection!" — you don't have a collection yet, you have pieces

  • Flat product photography on white background — that's Amazon, not luxury

  • Price reveals on the feed

  • "DM to order" — too early, you're building desire not taking orders

  • Comparing yourself to established jewelry brands

  • Using the word "affordable" ever


Food Content (Not Cringe)

What works:


The silent first bite. Close-up of the dish. Cut to you eating it. No talking. Your face says everything. Maybe a slow nod. Maybe you close your eyes. 10 seconds. Caption: the restaurant name and the dish.

The overhead pour. Film directly above as a sauce gets poured, a drink gets made, a plate gets assembled. No commentary. Just the visual ASMR of food being finished. This is the shot that goes viral.

The honest take. Not everything has to be positive. "this wasn't it" hits harder than forced enthusiasm about every meal. Being honest builds trust, and trust builds audience. Don't trash the restaurant — just be real. "beautiful space. food needs work."

The local guide. "3 places in Wynwood you're walking past." Not a listicle Reel with transitions — just you walking in, one shot of each dish, walking out. Minimal. Fast. Useful.

The chef moment. If the chef comes out, that's content. A 15-second clip of the chef plating or explaining one dish. You're not the host — you're the observer. You're bringing your audience into spaces they wouldn't normally access.

The counter seat. Always sit at the bar or chef's counter if you can. You see the kitchen. You see the process. Better content, better experience, and it looks cooler than a table.

What to avoid:


  • "OMG you HAVE to try this" in a high-pitched voice

  • Rating systems (7.3 out of 10) — that's a food critic, not a creative

  • Overly choreographed transitions between dishes

  • "Tag someone who needs to try this!!"

  • Eating on camera and doing a "mmmmm" reaction

  • Long monologues about the flavor profile

  • "Hidden gem" in every caption — if everything's a hidden gem, nothing is


Travel Content (Not Cringe)

What works:


The window shot. Plane window, car window, train window. A city appearing. No text needed. Maybe your hand holding a coffee in frame. This is the universal "I'm going somewhere" shot that never gets old if you don't overdo it.

The texture reel. A 15-second montage of textures from a city: the pavement, a door handle, a wall, a fabric, food on a plate, a drink, a street sign. No face. No narration. Just the feeling of a place through surfaces. This is very Virgil — he photographed textures constantly.

The "same thing, different city." You DJing in Tokyo vs. Miami. You eating the local version of a dish in three countries. Your emerald ring against different city backdrops. The recurring element across locations becomes your visual signature.

The night walk. 30 seconds of walking through a city at night. Street sounds. Neon. Movement. You don't even need to be visible. Just the vibe of being somewhere at night. Caption: the city name.

The return. Coming back to Miami after a trip. The skyline, the heat hitting you at the airport, your first meal back. "home." — that's a powerful recurring post.

What to avoid:


  • "Take me back" or "already missing this place"

  • Tourist-angle photos of landmarks everyone's seen

  • Packing videos or "what I'm bringing" content

  • Hotel room tours

  • "#blessed" or "#wanderlust" anything

  • Travel tips or "how to save money in [city]" — you're not a travel blogger


The Crossover Posts (Where It All Connects)

These are the posts that make people realize you're not just a food account or a music account. You're a world.

"studio dinner." You cooked something simple after a studio session. The plates, the laptop still open in the background, an emerald ring on your hand. Three pillars in one frame. Zero explanation.

"pre-show." Eating at a restaurant before a DJ set. The food, then cut to the venue. Two worlds in 20 seconds.

"sourcing." In Colombia. At a market. Emeralds on the table. Arepas in the other hand. Speakers playing vallenato in the background. Travel + jewelry + food + music in one moment that's just... your life.

"sunday." A slow montage: morning coffee, a vinyl playing, close-up of a ring you're designing, a simple meal. No hook. No CTA. Just a quiet day. This is the post that makes people feel like they know you.

Posting Rhythm

Don't think in terms of a content calendar. Think in terms of rhythm.

  • 3-4 Reels per week (alternating pillars naturally)

  • Daily stories when you're doing something (not forced daily updates)

  • 1 carousel per week max (only for Celadon process or food guides)

  • Nothing on days you have nothing — silence is better than filler


The goal is that when someone scrolls your feed, they see: food, music, emerald, travel, food, music, life moment. It should feel like flipping through someone's visual diary, not a marketing calendar.

The 3% Rule

Only 3% of your content should be "asking for something" — listen to my track, buy my jewelry, come to my show. The other 97% is just showing up and being interesting. If you're interesting enough, the 3% converts without resistance.

Virgil barely ever said "buy Off-White." He existed. People bought it because they wanted to be part of whatever he was part of. That's the play.

One Last Thing

The biggest anti-cringe move: don't talk about being a brand. Don't post about "building something." Don't share motivational quotes. Don't do "day 1 of 365" challenges. Don't make content about making content.

Just make things. Wear things. Eat things. Play things. Go places. The camera catches it. You post it. That's all it ever needs to be.
