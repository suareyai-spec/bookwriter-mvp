## 1. NEWS WRITING

### From *Writing and Reporting News* (Carole Rich)

**Focus & Structure:**
- State the focus (main idea) of every story in ONE sentence before writing. If you can't, you don't have a story yet.
- Answer the reader's question: "So what? Why should I care?"
- Use the "tell-a-friend" technique: How would you explain this to a friend? That's your lead.
- Write in conversational tone — as though telling someone the news, not reading from a textbook.
- Lead with the most important/interesting information first (inverted pyramid).
- Keep leads under 35 words. For mobile/digital delivery, brevity is critical.
- Every story needs a "nut graph" — a sentence or paragraph that tells the reader in a nutshell what the story is about and why it's newsworthy. Place it within the first 3-5 paragraphs.

**Lead Types:**
- **Summary lead**: Directly answers who, what, when, where, why, how. Best for hard news.
- **Feature/anecdotal lead**: Starts with a person, scene, or incident — then transitions to the nut graph.
- **Question lead**: Poses a question the reader wants answered (use sparingly).
- After the lead, place the strongest quote ("lead quote" / "augmenting quote") to back up the main idea.

**Story Body:**
- Support the focus with quotes, facts, and information — never wander from the focus.
- Show impact: explain how the news affects the reader. Answer "so what?"
- Provide context/background so readers understand the significance.
- Seek multiple sources and opposing viewpoints for balance and fairness.
- Use elaboration: supporting points, reactions, additional detail.
- End with future action, a strong closing quote, or new information — never repeat what was already said.

**Quotes & Attribution:**
- Quotes are the "spice" of the story, not the meat and potatoes. Use them to add color, not to convey basic facts.
- If you can say it better yourself, paraphrase. Only use direct quotes when they're vivid, emotional, or add voice.
- Attribute all quotes and non-obvious information. Attribution builds credibility.
- Place attribution after the first sentence in a multi-sentence quote, not at the end.
- Never tack on long explanations before or after quotes — use a transition instead.

**Writing Quality:**
- "Make me see" — write vividly enough that the reader can visualize the scene.
- Hard news = what happened. Feature = people affected by what happened.
- Use specific details, not generalizations.
- Every word must earn its place. Cut ruthlessly.

### From *The Associated Press Stylebook* (2019)

**Style Rules:**
- Write tight. Eliminate unnecessary words. Prefer short sentences and simple words.
- Use active voice. "The board approved the plan" not "The plan was approved by the board."
- Avoid jargon and bureaucratic language. Translate complex terms for general readers.
- Numbers: Spell out one through nine; use figures for 10 and above. Use % symbol with figures (53%).
- Titles: Capitalize formal titles before names, lowercase after or alone. Abbreviate standard titles (Dr., Gov., Sen.) before full names.
- Dates: Abbreviate months with dates (Jan. 15, Feb. 3) but spell out when standing alone. Never abbreviate March, April, May, June, July.
- Attribution: Use "said" — not "stated," "exclaimed," "noted," "shared." "Said" is invisible to readers.
- Never start a sentence with a numeral. Rewrite or spell it out.
- Avoid clichés, euphemisms, and loaded language.
- Use "more than" instead of "over" for quantities. Use "fewer" for countable items, "less" for bulk/quantity.
- Race/ethnicity: Use precise, respectful language. Report race only when relevant.
- Avoid alphabet soup — don't use abbreviations readers won't recognize.
- Split infinitives when needed for clarity. Writing naturally > rigid grammar rules.

### From *The Elements of Journalism* (Kovach & Rosenstiel)

**Core Principles:**
1. **Journalism's first obligation is to the truth.** Not "balance" — truth. Verify. Seek the best obtainable version of the truth.
2. **Its first loyalty is to citizens.** Not advertisers, not sources, not the organization.
3. **Its essence is a discipline of verification.** Objectivity is about METHOD, not the journalist's personal views. The process must be transparent, rigorous, and defensible.
4. **Maintain independence from those you cover.** Avoid conflicts of interest. Independence ≠ neutrality — it means freedom to pursue the truth.
5. **Monitor power and give voice to the voiceless.**
6. **Provide a forum for public criticism and compromise.**
7. **Make the significant interesting and relevant.** Don't dumb down — make complex things clear and engaging.
8. **Make news comprehensive and proportional.** Cover what matters, not just what gets clicks.
9. **Practitioners must exercise personal conscience.** Every journalist is responsible for the work they produce.
10. **Citizens have rights and responsibilities too.** They must demand quality and participate.

**On Truth & Verification:**
- Truth in journalism is a process, not a destination. It's built over time through reporting, correction, and context.
- Transparency of method is the modern form of objectivity. Show your work. Cite your sources. Explain how you know what you know.
- In a world of social media and rumors, journalists must note false claims, track their impact, and show why they should be disbelieved.

**On Relevance:**
- The most important skill is making significant information interesting and relevant to readers' lives.
- Avoid infotainment — the blurring of news and entertainment erodes trust.
- Quality content means depth + engagement. Chasing clicks with shallow content undermines long-term credibility.

## 2. FEATURE / LITERARY JOURNALISM

### From *Feature Writing: The Pursuit of Excellence* (Friedlander & Lee)

**Feature Story Principles:**
- Features focus on the **human element** — people, emotions, experiences — not just events.
- The best features make readers feel something: empathy, surprise, curiosity, outrage, joy.
- Feature ideas come from everywhere: overheard conversations, news briefs, trends, personal observation.
- A Dozen Lead Categories include: narrative, descriptive, contrast, question, quote, direct address, analogy, teaser, anecdote, staccato, literary allusion, parody.

**Structure:**
- Features don't use inverted pyramid. They use narrative arc: setup → complication → resolution.
- Begin with a compelling scene or character. Delay the nut graph if your opening is strong enough to sustain interest.
- Writing the body: alternate between scene/narrative and exposition/explanation. Weave quotes into the narrative naturally.
- Endings matter enormously in features. End with a memorable image, a full-circle callback, an emotional beat, or a telling quote. Never just trail off.

**Craft:**
- Show, don't tell. Use specific, concrete details instead of abstractions.
- Use all five senses in descriptions — not just sight.
- Gather 10x more material than you'll use. The best details come from deep reporting.
- Revise relentlessly. Good feature writing comes from rewriting, not first drafts.
- Read Pulitzer Prize-winning features to internalize rhythm and structure.

### From *The Art of Fact* (Kerrane & Yagoda)

**Literary Journalism Principles:**
- Literary journalism uses the techniques of fiction — scene, character, dialogue, point of view — to tell true stories.
- Immersion reporting: Spend enough time with subjects to capture authentic detail and voice.
- Scene-by-scene construction: Build the story through scenes rather than summary. Let moments unfold in real time.
- Record dialogue naturally. How people actually speak reveals character.
- Status details: Capture the specific objects, gestures, habits, and environments that reveal a person's social position and inner life.
- The best literary journalism has a **dual narrative**: the surface story (what happens) and the deeper story (what it means).
- Point of view matters: First person (reporter as participant), third person (reporter as observer), or omniscient (moving between perspectives). Choose deliberately.
- Compression is key: Even in long-form, every sentence must advance the story or deepen understanding. Cut the merely interesting to keep the essential.

## 3. PERSUASIVE / OPINION WRITING

### From *Thank You for Arguing* (Jay Heinrichs)

**Core Rhetorical Principles:**
- **Argument has three goals**: Stimulate emotion (pathos), change the audience's mind (logos), or win trust/admiration (ethos). The best persuasion uses all three.
- **Future tense is the language of persuasion.** Past tense assigns blame. Present tense values. Future tense makes choices and decisions. Always steer arguments toward the future.
- **Concession is powerful.** Agreeing with part of your opponent's argument defuses tension and makes you appear reasonable. Then redirect.
- **The audience decides.** Persuasion isn't about winning — it's about getting your audience to do what you want. Adapt your approach to THEIR values, emotions, and identity.

**Ethos (Character/Credibility):**
- Three traits of a credible speaker: **virtue** (shared values), **practical wisdom** (appears to know what they're talking about), **disinterest** (appears to have the audience's interests at heart, not their own).
- Admit mistakes early. It builds trust faster than defending yourself.
- Use the audience's language, references, and values — not your own.

**Logos (Logic/Reasoning):**
- Use the **enthymeme**: a logical sandwich where you build on a belief the audience already holds. "We should do X [choice] because of Y [commonplace the audience accepts]."
- Avoid logical fallacies: false dilemma, slippery slope, ad hominem, red herring, straw man, appeal to popularity, false cause.
- Concede and pivot: "You make a fair point about X. And that's exactly why we should Y."

**Pathos (Emotion):**
- Stories > statistics for emotional persuasion. Make it personal.
- Anger motivates action. Desire motivates loyalty. Fear motivates caution. Choose the emotion that serves your goal.
- Humor disarms and creates connection. Self-deprecating humor builds likability.

**For Writing:**
- **Figures of speech** are rhetorical power tools: metaphor, antithesis (contrasting ideas), anaphora (repetition at start of sentences), tricolon (groups of three).
- Start with what the audience cares about, not what you want to say.
- The strongest position in a sentence is the END. Place your most important word or idea there.
- In persuasive essays: Start by defining the issue on your terms (framing). Present your strongest evidence. Anticipate and defuse counterarguments. End with a clear call to action.

## 4. SEO & DIGITAL CONTENT

### From *SEO 2025* (Adam Clarke)

**Content for Search:**
- Google ranks based on 5 core principles: **Trust, Authority, Expertise, Relevance, User Experience**.
- Content must be written for humans first, search engines second. Google's AI (RankBrain, BERT) now understands natural language and intent.
- **Keyword intent is everything.** Match content type to search intent:
  - Informational: "How to..." → provide clear, thorough answers
  - Commercial investigation: "Best X" → provide unbiased comparisons with pros/cons
  - Transactional: "Buy X" → make the purchase path clear and simple
- Use **semantic keywords** (related terms) naturally throughout content. Don't just repeat one keyword — cover the topic comprehensively.
- **E-E-A-T** (Experience, Expertise, Authoritativeness, Trustworthiness): Google rewards content from credible, experienced authors. Show credentials. Cite sources. Include real expertise.

**On-Page SEO for Articles:**
- Use clear, descriptive H1 tags with primary keyword.
- Use H2-H6 subheadings to structure content logically.
- Write compelling meta titles (under 60 chars) and meta descriptions (under 160 chars) that encourage clicks.
- Include internal links to related content. Link to authoritative external sources.
- Ensure fast page speed and mobile-responsive design.
- Use structured data (schema markup) to help Google understand content.
- Fresh content signals relevance. Update existing content regularly.

**Content Marketing for SEO:**
- Answer the questions your audience is actually searching for.
- Create "10x content" — content that is 10 times better than anything currently ranking for your target keyword.
- Longer, more comprehensive content tends to rank higher (aim for 1,500-3,000+ words for pillar content).
- Use tools like AnswerThePublic, BuzzSumo, and Google's "People Also Ask" to find content ideas.
- Build topical authority: create clusters of related content around core themes.

## 5. CONTENT MARKETING

### From *They Ask, You Answer* (Marcus Sheridan)

**The Core Philosophy:**
- **They Ask, You Answer**: Become the most trusted voice in your industry by honestly answering every question your customers ask.
- 70% of the buying decision is made BEFORE a prospect contacts you. Your content IS your sales team.
- "If a customer has a question, you have an obligation to answer it, honestly and thoroughly."

**The Big 5 Content Types** (topics that drive the most traffic, leads, and sales):
1. **Pricing/Cost**: Address pricing openly. "How much does X cost?" is the #1 searched question in almost every industry. Even if your answer is "it depends," explain the factors.
2. **Problems**: Honestly discuss the problems, downsides, and limitations of your product/service. This builds massive trust.
3. **Versus/Comparisons**: Compare yourself to competitors fairly. "X vs Y" content ranks well and captures buyers in the decision phase.
4. **Reviews/Best-in-Class**: Review products in your space, including competitors. Be the unbiased resource. "Best X for Y" content drives high-intent traffic.
5. **"Best of" Lists**: Curate the best options in your industry. Become the go-to resource.

**Writing Principles:**
- Write like a teacher, not a marketer. Education > promotion.
- Address the elephant in the room. The things you're afraid to talk about are exactly what customers want to know.
- **Disarmament**: Proactively address your weaknesses. "We're not the right fit if..." builds more trust than pretending to be perfect.
- Every article should make the reader think: "This company genuinely wants to help me make the best decision, even if it's not with them."
- Use "Assignment Selling": Create content so good that sales teams can assign it as "homework" before meetings.

### From *Content Strategy for the Web* (Halvorson & Rach)

**Strategic Principles:**
- Content without strategy is just stuff. Before creating content, define: Who is it for? What does it need to accomplish? How will it be maintained?
- **Core strategy statement**: Define the intersection of what your organization wants to say and what your audience needs to hear.
- Audit existing content ruthlessly. Most organizations have far too much mediocre content and not enough great content.
- Every piece of content should serve a specific user need AND a business goal simultaneously.

**Content Quality:**
- Useful > clever. Clear > creative. Helpful > promotional.
- Content must be: findable, readable, understandable, actionable, shareable.
- Write for scanners: use headings, bullets, short paragraphs, bold key phrases.
- Voice and tone should be consistent across all content but adapt to context (a 404 page and a product page need different tones).

**Governance & Sustainability:**
- Plan for content lifecycle: creation → publication → maintenance → archival/removal.
- Someone must OWN content strategy. Without a content owner, quality degrades.
- "Reduce, reuse, recycle" applies to content. Repurpose top performers; retire underperformers.

## 6. GENERAL WRITING CRAFT

### From *Everybody Writes* (Ann Handley)

**Writing Process:**
- **Writing is a habit, not an art.** Write daily. Even 30 minutes a day beats a 5-hour Saturday session.
- **Embrace the ugly first draft.** Get ideas down, then revise. Perfection on the first pass is impossible and paralyzing.
- **Follow a Writing GPS**: Goal (what's the purpose?), Reframe (put the reader first), Seek out data/examples, Organize, Write draft, Rewrite, Edit, Publish.
- **Start with "Dear Mom..."** If you're stuck, write as if explaining to someone who knows nothing about the topic. Then delete "Dear Mom."
- **The more the think, the easier the ink.** Research and thinking before writing saves time during writing.
- Swap places with your reader. Ask: "What's in it for them?" — not "What do I want to say?"

**Sentence-Level Craft:**
- **Place the most important words at the beginning of each sentence.** Front-load meaning.
- **Use familiar yet surprising analogies.** They make abstract concepts concrete and memorable.
- **Show, don't tell.** "Revenue increased 40% in six months" > "The company did very well."
- **Ditch weakling verbs.** Replace "is," "was," "has" with active, specific verbs.
- **Ditch adverbs** (except when they change meaning). If you need an adverb, your verb is too weak.
- **Use clichés only once in a blue moon.** They signal lazy thinking.
- **Use real words.** Avoid jargon, Frankenwords ("incentivize," "synergize"), and corporate-speak.
- **Active voice > passive voice.** "We launched the product" not "The product was launched."

**Content Quality Formula:**
- **Utility × Inspiration × Empathy = Quality Content**
  - Utility: Clearly help readers do something that matters to them
  - Inspiration: Fresh, different, well-crafted — feels like it could only come from you
  - Empathy: Relentlessly focus on the reader's needs, pains, and desires
- If any factor is zero, the content is zero.

**Headlines & Structure:**
- A good lede (lead) invites you to the party. A good kicker (ending) makes you wish you could stay longer.
- **Approach writing like teaching.** The best content helps someone learn or do something new.
- **Keep it simple but not simplistic.** Respect the reader's intelligence while making complex things accessible.
- **Be rabid about readability.** Short paragraphs. White space. Subheadings. Bullet points. All increase engagement.
- Set word count goals, not time goals. "500 words" is more concrete than "write for an hour."

**Publishing & Ethics:**
- Tell the truth. Always. Content marketing built on dishonesty is a house of cards.
- Cite sources as you write. Attribution builds credibility and trust.
- Fact-check everything. Your reputation rides on accuracy.
- Curate ethically: add value when sharing others' work. Link. Credit. Don't just aggregate.

## QUICK REFERENCE: ACTIONABLE RULES FOR AI ARTICLE GENERATION

### Structure
1. Start with a compelling lead (summary, anecdotal, or question-based)
2. Include a nut graph within the first 3-5 paragraphs
3. Organize with clear H2/H3 subheadings for scannability
4. End with a strong kicker: callback, forward-looking statement, or memorable image
5. Use the inverted pyramid for news; narrative arc for features

### Writing Quality
6. Write in conversational tone — like explaining to a smart friend
7. Use active voice and strong, specific verbs
8. Show, don't tell — use concrete details, data, and examples
9. Front-load sentences with the most important information
10. Keep paragraphs short (2-4 sentences max for digital)
11. Cut ruthlessly — every word must earn its place

### Reader Focus
12. Always answer: "So what? Why should the reader care?"
13. Write with pathological empathy — see the world through the reader's eyes
14. Address the questions readers actually have, not what you want to talk about
15. Provide context and background so readers can understand significance
16. Make the complex clear without dumbing it down

### Credibility & Trust
17. Cite sources and attribute information
18. Address counterarguments and limitations honestly
19. Show expertise through depth, not jargon
20. Be transparent about methodology and sources of information
21. Proactively address weaknesses and "elephants in the room"

### SEO & Digital
22. Match content to search intent (informational, commercial, transactional)
23. Use semantic keywords naturally throughout
24. Write compelling meta titles (<60 chars) and descriptions (<160 chars)
25. Structure with H1/H2/H3 hierarchy
26. Internal link to related content; external link to authoritative sources
27. Aim for comprehensive coverage — answer every related question
28. Update content regularly to maintain freshness

### Persuasion
29. Use the audience's values, language, and frame of reference
30. Lead with the audience's pain point, then offer the solution
31. Use stories and anecdotes to make points memorable
32. Apply the rule of three (tricolon) for rhythm and memorability
33. Concede valid opposing points to build credibility before making your case
34. End with a clear, actionable takeaway or call to action
